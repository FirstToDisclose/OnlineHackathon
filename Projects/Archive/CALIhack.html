Topic:

 

Discuss/Brainstorm CALI Legal Hackathon as Mini-Session Inside Future CALI Planning Session, Saturday June 20, 12pm Mountain / 2pm Eastern

Description:

Using Mass-Scale Collaboration approach to engage all participants directly in brainstorming session on potential future CALI Hackathon.  Process is to have attendees operate in multiple rounds using easy/quick bursts of activity.  The rounds start with broader ideation and narrow down with each sequential round.  The topic for each round is ideally based on a context-appropriate framing in view of the prior rounds input or feedback. Twitter is a good fit for this.  The process has also been shown to work using webforms that provide real-time reporting via an online database or spreadsheet.  

Concept of Rounds:

The first round could be a basis framework like:

  • What topics should a CALI Legal Hackathon focus on? (eg Access to Justice, Legal Education, Contracts and Transactions, etc)

After a short period (maybe 5 minute? or 2 minutes could be faster and better) for people to propose ideas, the round would end.  Then the RATINGS of the ideas proposed would begin.  

The MIT mass-scale process uses input and feedback as the two functional parts of each round.  For this CALIhack implementation, the ratings would provide feedback needed to conclude each round.  The first round starts with “input” (ie “what topic”?) and concludes with “rating” the ideas proposed.  

The second round could be to discuss what aspect of the top topic should be explored.  For example, if Access to Justice was the clear top topic then the next round could focus on what projects or problems the Hackathon should address.  Or if the top topic related to hackathon on better ways to use MOOCS in classrooms then the next round could ask which existing or emerging MOOC platforms people want to focus on, or to ask what types of projects to hack on a MOOC would people propose (eg use a MOOC to educate flipped classroom across several law schools and build-in a mini-class project enabling students to work on teams and engage with students on other teams in their own law school or other law schools to discuss how they approached the projects and/or to provide feedback on the projects of other teams and to receives feedback from teams and/or feedback from volunteer lawyers or other professors, etc.).  

If there were a few top rated topics proposed and no clear single top topic, then the second round could explore various aspects of the top topics.  For example, the second round could go directly to ratings based on a curation of the top topics arising from the first round and could ask for rating on a few scales for each proposed topic, such as 1-5 star rating on each of the following criteria: a) Relationship of this Topic to Academic Priorities, b) Fun-Factor/Popularity of this Topic, c). Relationship of this Topic to CALI Priorities, etc.  In this way, the result of Round 2 would be a quick read-out of the aspects of each top topic from round one that are deemed important, urgent, cool, strategic, innovative, etc.  Like this:

Round 2: Rate Proposed Hackathon Topics:

  1. #Access2Justice

b) 1-5 Stars: Fun-Factor/Popularity of this Topic,

c) 1-5 Stars: Relationship of this Topic to CALI Priorities

  1. #Slack

Using Slack for Legal Education Classrooms and Administration

a) 1-5 Stars: Relationship of this Topic to Academic Priorities,

b) 1-5 Stars: Fun-Factor/Popularity of this Topic,

c) 1-5 Stars: Relationship of this Topic to CALI Priorities

  1. #MOOCS

Developing MOOCS for Legal Education

a) 1-5 Stars: Relationship of this Topic to Academic Priorities,

b) 1-5 Stars: Fun-Factor/Popularity of this Topic,

c) 1-5 Stars: Relationship of this Topic to CALI Priorities

Why Twitter For Rounds?

If we use Twitter (as is recommended to pull this off in no time and with no budget but using only existing stuff we have laying around already) then the way we do that is by presenting each idea proposed on a webpage and making them clickable with a unique hashtag.  Literally what we do, is a “five-star rating hack” developed for the ABA-MIT Appathon (but not implemented) whereby each idea is presented with a set of 5 icons representing 1-5 star rating.  Each icon has embedded inside it a link.  The link auto-generates a Tweet.  The auto-generated Tweet has embedded within it the hashtags for the event and engagement (#CALICon15 and #CALIhack respectively) and for the object being rated (eg the proposed topic or other idea, such as #MOOC or #Access2Justice, etc) and for the rating on the object (eg #2stars or 4stars, etc).  The full syntax of the link looks like this:

Integrating, configuring, sequencing use of Twitter, Slack, an online database or Google Spreadsheets, etc will require some thought.  I plan to chat with Jason tonight about how best to do this for tomorrow.  Here are some thoughts on use of Slack:

One potential good use of Slack for tomorrow could be some sub-channels related to ideas or themes arising from one or more rounds that people really want to explore more deeply.  Based on prior use of this type of approach with groups, it is predictable that people will want to talk about particular projects, and some may want to talk about particular technologies that are much narrower than general or broader discussion of the happenings of an entire round.  Some people may also (probably) want a way to discuss next steps after the CALI 2015 conference, and there is always a contingent of people interested in how we arranged the MIT mass-scale collaboration approach and to talk about alternative methods and approaches (eg DIGG, Google Moderator, and a slew of other potential alternatives).  

Another good use of the Slack org could be to create a channel or channels for each round.  So, one channel could be CALIhackTweetsRound1. In Twitter Channel friendly syntax one would have a shorter channel name all in lowercase, such as #discussround1 followed by #discussround2 and so on.  It is also possible to integrate Twitter feeds to Slack.  I think I remember this can be focused on a hashtag.  If so, then each round could also have an accompanying twitter channel such as #tweetround1 and #tweetround 2 to make it easy for everybody to review each tweet created for each round inside of Slack.  The channel where all tweets are dumped for each round *could* be the same as where the ideas are discussed for each round but it would likely be a cleaner interface and simpler user experience if there were a separate channel for each discussion apart from the channel for Tweets.  The flow is Slack discussion can be broken badly by the sudden introduction of a bunch of tweets that have nothing to do with the question or thread one is in the middle of replying to and trying to engage on.  So, the tweet channels should probably not be the same channel where people discuss the ideas.  One approach could be:

Slack Channels For Round 1:

#discussround1

#tweetround1

Slack Channels For Round 2:

#discussround2

#tweetround2

Slack Channels For Round 3:

#discussround3

#tweetround3

It is not easy to use slack as a direct method of the ratings or to hack directly inside the Slack code-base to configure and sequence a multi-round large group engagement that auto-feeds subsequent rounds with results from prior rounds or that provides ratings services in a fine-tuned manner or the other aspects of the MIT approach.  But Slack is a great place to chat so it should be used to chat about topics related to CALIhack and the discussion that may take place on Saturday.
